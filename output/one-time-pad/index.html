one-time pad

A cryptographic technique that uses a single-use random key to encrypt and decrypt messages, ensuring perfect secrecy.

Description

The one-time pad (OTP) is a type of encryption that is theoretically unbreakable when used correctly. It involves combining plaintext with a random key (or pad) that is the same length as the plaintext and is used only once. To encrypt a message, each character of the plaintext is combined with the corresponding character of the key, typically using modular arithmetic. The resulting ciphertext is completely random and bears no statistical relationship to the plaintext, making it impossible to decrypt without the key. For security, the key must be truly random, at least as long as the message, never reused, and kept secret. The one-time pad was famously used during World War II and has been the subject of extensive theoretical research in cryptography. However, its practical application is limited due to the challenges of key generation, distribution, and management, as well as the requirement for both sender and receiver to securely share the key beforehand.

Examples

  • Used in diplomatic communications during World War II.
  • Theoretically used in secure messaging applications.

Additional Information

  • Requires secure key exchange methods to ensure confidentiality.
  • Not practical for large-scale or frequent communications due to key management issues.

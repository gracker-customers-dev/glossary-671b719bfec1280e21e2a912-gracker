Bell-LaPadula Model

A security model that defines access controls based on classification levels.

Description

The Bell-LaPadula Model is a formal security model primarily used to enforce access control in government and military environments. It was developed in the 1970s by David Bell and Leonard LaPadula. The model is based on the concept of multi-level security and is designed to prevent unauthorized access to classified information. It operates on two main properties: the Simple Security Property (no read up) and the *-Property (no write down). The Simple Security Property prohibits a subject at a lower security level from reading data at a higher level, while the *-Property prevents a subject from writing data to a lower security level, thus protecting sensitive information from being leaked to less secure environments. The model emphasizes confidentiality, ensuring that sensitive information is only accessed by users with the appropriate clearance. The Bell-LaPadula Model is widely used in systems that require stringent control over data access, particularly in military and intelligence applications where protecting classified information is crucial.

Examples

  • A military personnel with a Secret clearance cannot access Top Secret documents.
  • A government employee with a Confidential clearance cannot share sensitive information with individuals holding only Unclassified clearance.

Additional Information

  • The Bell-LaPadula Model primarily focuses on confidentiality and does not address integrity.
  • It is often implemented in systems that require strict adherence to security policies, such as classified military databases.
